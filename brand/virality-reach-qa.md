**What's the post that got you the most impressions ever, what was it about, and why do you think it blew up?**

The post that got the highest impressions for us was about the new Andromeda update from Meta. A lot of the people who follow us know us because we're in the Meta space, we do Meta ads, and that update was a big change in how people should act. So we created a quick breakdown of it and gave a lot of value: our thoughts on it, what it meant, all packaged as a quick 13-page carousel guide. Just breaking it down, how it works, what happens, what this means for you. Trying to help people.

Obviously we weren't the ones creating that update, we were just breaking it down. And I think it's the law of reciprocity. When you give someone something valuable, they trust you more and feel the need to give back. That was really good for us. It helped us gain a lot of followers, a lot of impressions, and a lot of traffic to our website.

**When you trendjack something, what's the exact process? How do you find the trend, how fast do you move, and what does the post look like?**

It's not like you wake up one morning and decide "alright, I'm going to trendjack stuff today." That's usually not how it works. How it actually works is you're online in your space most of the day, so you're aware of the changes happening in the industry. Then once a trend starts, say AI ads are suddenly everywhere, you take action on that trend and display your take or your view on it in a way that's helpful to others. That's my trendjack process.

If I had to formalize it, I'd note out the key players in the industry: the people who are an authority in the space I'm in, plus the competitors. There's no problem admitting we compete with these guys, it's healthy competition, we push each other. In our case, for Meta ads, I'd have maybe 10 people annotated who we feel are authorities and competitors, and we keep tabs on what they're saying and what topics they're talking about. That's the process: keep tabs on the important people in the industry that your customers also pay attention to.

**Is there a specific format that consistently goes more viral than others for you? Short take, personal story, system breakdown, something else?**

For us it's more about the novelty of the information, how unique the thing we post is, than the format itself. But what normally goes super viral for us is lead magnets. And if you combine lead magnets with trends or novel stuff, for example the Meta Andromeda update, AI images, AI ads, that usually goes well.

It also works when we make it broad. When something goes viral for us, it's the lead magnet combined with something broad. We don't care as much about who we're targeting, we make it as broad as possible so it reaches anyone who could be in the space: competitors, people who couldn't actually buy the offer, everyone. Broad plus a lead magnet spirals more often. Recently, articles have been doing well for us too. Articles are pretty recent in the history of X, so we'll see, but the early signs are good.

**What's the difference between a post that gets a lot of likes from other agency owners versus one that actually reaches new people outside your bubble?**

I think it comes back to the first line. The job of the first line is to qualify the reader. By qualify, I mean the first line has to mean something to the specific person you intend to reach.

If I want to go super viral, I don't make the first line too specific. I'd say something like "ChatGPT images are crazy good for static ads." That could be for literally anyone interested in static ads. But if you want to be more specific and not go as wide, you narrow it down, something like "ChatGPT images are great for [a specific type of] brands right now." That's much more specific, you won't go as viral, but you'll attract more people who are actually qualified for you. Same if you make newsletters for info products, you say something specific to them.

You've got to balance it. Not all-out broad, not all-out narrow. Find a mix, and you can actually distribute that mix across the week. That's what I've found to work.

**You mentioned the first line decides everything. What makes a first line stop someone mid-scroll versus one that gets ignored?**

Two things. First, the line has to draw curiosity. A good rule of thumb is to ask yourself: is the first line actually making them want to read the second line? Second, does the first line evoke an emotion? Does it make someone feel something?

A trick for this, and I don't want to say rage-baiting, is to be a bit polemic with your first line. That's why you see those first lines like "Claude just killed email marketing" or whatever. That always goes viral because it generates a reaction, and it's funny. I personally don't create those types of posts, but I've seen them work, and there's a reason they work: they generate emotion.

**When a post starts taking off in the first hour, what do you do to push it further? Do you comment on it, engage with replies, anything specific?**

On both Twitter and LinkedIn, the way I boost a post is by reposting it. With X articles specifically, what's been proven to work is quote-tweeting your own article, especially about 6 hours after you post. I love doing that. If you plan the quote tweet well and actually have something valuable to say to push people toward the article, it performs really well and gets a lot of impressions. So the main move is: post, wait about 6 hours, then repost or quote-tweet it.

The other thing that helps is replying to every single person who comments, on both LinkedIn and Twitter. That's a rule I think is super important.

**What's the biggest mistake you see people make when they're trying to go viral, the thing that actually kills reach before it starts?**

The thing that actually kills reach is not letting the post sit for a few hours and posting something else right after it. You've got to leave it be and go from there.

**Can someone manufacture virality, or does it just happen? What's actually in your control and what isn't?**

You can manufacture virality. I think that's basically what lead magnets are. When we post something and ask people to comment a keyword to get a resource, we're signaling the algorithm: hey, people are commenting on this, it must be important, it must be valuable, it must be something people are in the app for.

That said, Twitter and LinkedIn don't really love it. The goal of these algorithms is to keep you on the app as long as possible, and I don't know how much they like people exiting the app to receive a message about a resource. When we run a lead magnet DM, we're pulling people out of the app.

Compare that to an article. If your article is really good, people stay on it longer, which signals the algorithm that people like this resource. So when we ask people to like and comment to get sent a resource or a video, we're helping the algorithm understand that people are liking this, people are commenting on this, this is good stuff, share it to more people.

**If someone has never had a post break 10K impressions, what's the one thing they should change first?**

Look at people in your industry and the posts of theirs that reached 10K impressions, and do the same thing with a couple of changes. I'm not saying copy them. Understand what topics they talk about and which type of posts are getting them a lot of impressions, do your version of that, and start testing from there.

And you can do one really valuable lead magnet per day on both Twitter and LinkedIn. You should have the time for that, especially if you want to grow.
