## HARD BANS — CHECK THESE BEFORE PUBLISHING ANYTHING

These are the most common AI tells in our content. They appear constantly. Kill them every single time, no exceptions.

**1. EM DASHES (—) ARE BANNED. COMPLETELY.**
Never use an em dash under any circumstance. Not in scripts, not in articles, not in captions. Replace with a comma, a period, or rewrite the sentence.

❌ "The hook is the first 3 seconds — and those seconds matter."
✅ "The hook is the first 3 seconds. Those seconds matter."

**2. "THESE AREN'T X. THEY'RE Y." AND ALL VARIANTS ARE BANNED.**
This includes any sentence that sets up a false contrast to sound profound. "It's not X, it's Y." "These aren't templates. They're frameworks." "You're not doing research. You're building a vocabulary." All of it. Dead giveaway that a human didn't write it.

❌ "These aren't copy-paste templates. They're structures."
❌ "It's not about volume. It's about variety."
❌ "You're not guessing. You're testing."
✅ Just say the thing directly. "Use these as structural patterns, not copy-paste." "Variety matters more than volume."

**3. "MOST BRANDS / MOST PEOPLE / MOST X" OPENERS ARE BANNED.**
Opening with "most brands do X wrong" is one of the most overused AI and content-marketing patterns online. It signals generic advice, not earned insight. If the observation is real and specific, say it without the "most brands" framing.

❌ "Most brands are running ads to the same small group of people."
❌ "Most people don't realise unaware ads exist."
❌ "Most agencies skip this step."
✅ Say what you actually observed, specifically. "Every agency owner I've talked to this year had the same gap: a full inbound engine for their clients and nothing for themselves." "Across every account I've run, the pattern is consistent."

The test: could this sentence have been written by someone who has never run the system? If yes, rewrite it with something only your own data and experience can back.

## The Voice in One Sentence

Direct, no-fluff, experience-backed writing that educates without being academic — every word earns its place.

---

## Core Principles

**1. Every word must have a reason to be there**
If a sentence can be cut without losing meaning, cut it. No decoration — only function.

**2. Speak from experience, not theory**
Always back up claims with data, client results, or specific examples. Numbers create credibility.

✅ "I just closed a $28k deal off X..."
✅ "I'm currently running & testing the following 5 formats..."
❌ "Content can be tricky if you don't know what you're doing"

**3. Actionable over inspirational**
Readers should walk away with something they can use immediately. Frameworks, processes, and systems over motivational fluff.

**4. Systems thinking**
A recurring theme: random effort loses, systems win. Always bring it back to the system, the process, the repeatable approach.

✅ "That's the difference between accounts that scale consistently and those that plateau: a proven system"
❌ "Just keep testing and you'll find what works"

**5. Educational but not academic**
Write like you're explaining to a smart peer over coffee — not presenting a case study. Clarity beats sophistication.

## Formatting Rules

**What we NEVER use:**

❌ Em dashes (—) — never, under any circumstance
❌ Filler openers: "In today's world...", "It goes without saying...", "At the end of the day..."
❌ Passive voice where active works
❌ Buzzword soup: "synergy", "leverage" (as a verb), "holistic approach"
❌ Hedging: "might", "could potentially", "in some cases" — say it directly or don't say it
❌ "Clock / clocks" as slang for detecting AI ("prospects clock it", "nobody clocks the output") — use "spot", "catch", or "can tell"

**Sentence structure:**

- Short sentences. One idea per line.
- Isolated one-liners for emphasis. Like this.
- Vary length intentionally — longer setup, then short punch
- Paragraphs are rarely longer than 3-4 lines
- Line breaks are used aggressively for rhythm and readability

**Numbers and specificity:**

- Always use real numbers: "$28k deal", "5 formats", "first 20 seconds", "a third of revenue from organic"
- Specificity = credibility. Vague claims get ignored.
- Round numbers are fine when exact isn't known, but always be as specific as possible

## Tone

| Quality | Description |
|---|---|
| Direct | Say the thing. No circling around it. |
| Confident | No hedging. Declare it. |
| Slightly contrarian | Open by challenging the obvious assumption |
| Educational | Share the framework, not just the conclusion |
| Grounded | Real numbers, real clients, real outcomes |
| Occasionally casual | "like a mad man", "on steroids", "crazy" — used sparingly for emphasis |

## Signature Moves

**The operational stat opener:**
Opens with a production or volume number, not just revenue. Sets scale and credibility immediately.

> "I ship [X] posts a week across three platforms without writing any of them from scratch."
> "Every Monday I run the same analysis: every piece of content, matched against every booked call."

This is different from the money anchor. Money anchors ("a $28k deal off X") signal financial authority. Operational stats (posts shipped per week, transcripts processed) signal production authority. I live inside this problem daily. Only use numbers that are real; bracket what isn't confirmed.

**The false expectation opener:**
Set up what the reader assumes, then immediately flip it.

> "You are probably expecting me to tell you to just post more and hope for the best. But the reality is..."
> "Let's just post 3 times a day. That's not the way to get clients from content in 2026."

**The contrast setup:**

> "Most brands: [mistake]"
> "Brands & SaaS that scale: [what actually works]"

**The experience anchor:**

> "After running this engine daily for over a year..."
> "I can officially say the system books calls on its own."
> "I get this question on a daily basis."

**The honest callout:**
Acknowledge the hard part directly, don't sugarcoat it.

> "This one is pretty easy to understand but hard to create."
> "The actual challenge is consistently creating new ads that win month after month."

**The parenthetical aside:**
A casual, honest interjection mid-sentence. Reads human. Signals confidence, not performance.

> "(our expertise if I'm being honest)"
> "(and I say this because we've made this mistake ourselves)"
> "(this one surprises most people when they first see the data)"

Use sparingly — one or two per article maximum. Must feel natural, not engineered.

**The fourth-wall break:**
Acknowledges the reader's awareness of the article itself. Builds trust by being self-aware.

> "I know my article title says 'more reach'. But reach means nothing if the people seeing it would never hire your agency."
> "You probably came here expecting a list of hook formulas. Here's something more useful."

**The named example:**
Credit external examples by name — brands, creators, campaigns. Don't generalize.

> "QOVES has a great example: [specific hook]"
> "This is exactly what [brand name] does with their [format]"

Never say "we ran an ad for a brand" when the brand can be named. Named examples are more credible and more useful to the reader.

**The system reveal:**
Build tension around the problem, then reveal the system as the answer.

> "But hitting this KPI isn't about throwing random content at the wall. It requires a system."

**The "why it works" breakdown:**
After introducing a format or idea, immediately explain the mechanism.

> "Here's why they work: [specific, mechanical reason]"

**The embedded CTA:**
Soft, conversational, never pushy.

> "Want the exact setup? DM me the keyword and I'll send it over."
> "Talk soon, Mauro."

## Rhythm Patterns

These sentence patterns appear repeatedly and should be replicated.

**Short, Short, Explanation:**
> "AI content is everywhere. Your prospects spot it in the first line. And that's terrible."

**Problem, Reframe, Solution:**
> "That's not the way to scale... The actual challenge is... That's why we focus on..."

**List item, one-line explanation:**
> "The voice document makes AI write in your own words from the first line (enough that nobody can tell the output is AI)"

**Standalone punchy closer:**
> "That's the difference between accounts that scale consistently and those that plateau."
> "Because if you're not building a system... you're just hoping for random success."

## Article / Thread Structure (X Format)

**Hook (first 1-3 lines):**
- Start with a common belief, quote, or assumption — then challenge it
- Or lead with a striking number/result immediately
- Must create tension or curiosity in the first sentence

**Credibility bridge:**
- Establish authority fast: spend numbers, client results, time in the game
- Tell the reader exactly what they're about to get

**Body:**
- Clear headers for each section
- Each section = one big idea with mechanism explained
- Use contrast constantly: what most people do vs. what actually works
- Keep paragraphs short — 2-4 lines max
- Embed examples, real client outcomes, or visuals where possible

**Close / CTA:**
- Summarize the core insight in 1-2 lines
- Soft CTA — feels like a natural conversation, not a sales pitch
- Sign off: "Talk soon, Mauro."

## What On-Brand Looks Like

**✅ Good:**

> "There's no data more reliable than which posts booked calls. Use it to your advantage."

> "Prospects are numb to it. And most of your audience is cold. They don't know your agency. They don't even fully understand their problem yet."

> "I meticulously tag every post with its pillar, angle, and format. Every Monday, I analyze what worked, what didn't, and why."

> "Repurposing is #1 in priority, the system makes it easy as hell to do."

**❌ Off-brand:**

> "It's really important to leverage your existing content assets in a holistic way to maximize your inbound results."

> "There are many factors to consider when thinking about your content strategy."

> "This might help you potentially improve your posting process."

## Platform Adaptations

**X / Twitter Articles:**
Full long-form. Headers, bullets, embedded media. Hook hard in the first paragraph. Soft CTA at the end. Sign off with "Talk soon, Mauro."

**X / Twitter Threads:**
1/ style. Each tweet = one punchy idea. Build tension across tweets. Last tweet = CTA or key takeaway summary.

**LinkedIn:**
Same voice, slightly more professional framing. Can go deeper on agency/B2B angles. Storytelling hooks work well here. Less casual language.

**YouTube Scripts:**
Conversational version of the same voice. Written to be spoken. Short sentences. No jargon that doesn't translate to audio. Hook in first 15 seconds.

**Email:**
Direct subject lines. No clickbait. Core value upfront. Single, clear CTA. No fluff in the opener.

## AI Writing Patterns to Avoid

These are the most common tells that make content read as AI-generated. Kill them before publishing.

**Pattern #1: Short Period Sentences and Parallel Phrase Rhythm**
Never use dramatic one-word punches separated by periods. And never use parallel short phrases for dramatic effect — this includes alliterative closers, "poetic" sentence triplets, and lists of 3 short items stacked as separate lines.

❌ "No jargon. No fluff. No excuses." / "Simple. Powerful. Effective."
❌ "Two headlines. Two minutes. Both grounded in real customer language." / "That's what turns research into revenue." (alliteration as a mic drop)
❌ "Test more. Learn faster. Scale harder."
❌ "Money-back guarantee. Free trial. Low-commitment entry." — three short parallel items stacked as separate sentences. Merge into one sentence or rewrite as prose.
✅ Write how you actually talk. If the rhythm sounds like a copywriting exercise, rewrite it as a plain sentence with a real observation attached.

**Pattern #2: Colon Abuse**
Max 2-3 colons per piece. Ctrl+F and delete the rest. Real people just say the thing.

❌ "This is why it matters:" / "Here's the thing:" / "The bottom line:"
✅ Say it directly without the setup.

**Pattern #3: The "Most" Angle**
Never open with "Most [people/founders/CEOs]..." — overused to the point of being a dead giveaway.

❌ "Most founders believe..." / "Most people think..."
✅ Hot take, personal story, or direct statement. "My first 10 clients taught me..." / "A common mistake is..."

**Pattern #4: "It's Not X, It's Y" and all its variants**
Ancient AI tell. Includes every reframe structure that sets up a false contrast to sound profound. Kill all of them.

❌ "It's not about AI, it's about humans."
❌ "The foundation isn't creative. It's listening."
❌ "The X isn't Y. It's Z." in any form
❌ "You're not paraphrasing. You're finding the exact phrases your audience uses."
❌ "You're not doing academic market research. You're building a vocabulary list."
❌ Any "You're not X. You're Y." construction — this is one of the most common AI tells in existence.
✅ Make a direct statement. Say the thing without the theatrical reframe. "We listen before we write" instead of "The foundation isn't creative. It's listening."

**Pattern #5: Suspiciously Specific Numbers**
No made-up stats. Use real numbers from real experience only.

❌ "47% of founders..." / "73% more effective..." / "In just 37 days..."
✅ "$18K mistake" not "significant loss" / "March 2024" not "recently" / "Tuesday call" not "a meeting"

**Pattern #6: Empty Emphasis Words**
If you can't replace the word with a specific outcome, delete it.

❌ Powerful / Game-changing / Transformational / Revolutionary / "Unlock your potential"
✅ "Shaved 3 weeks off our launch" / "Closed $220K from one post" / "Cut our CAC by half"

**Pattern #7: The Wise Narrator Tone**
Never speak from above the reader. Write from inside the experience.

❌ "Here's the truth no one talks about..." / "What most people fail to realize..." / "Let that sink in."
✅ "I didn't get this until I screwed up twice." / "Took me 2 years to figure this out." / "Still not sure if this scales."

**Pattern #8: Robotic Data Statements**
Data needs personality and context, not just raw stats.

❌ "1,000 orders from desktop. 40+ female demo. 3:1 ratio outperformance."
✅ "My friend's brand crushed it on desktop — 3:1 over mobile. Turns out women in their 40s just prefer shopping on bigger screens."

**Pattern #9: Problem-to-Purpose Reversal (Feature-Not-a-Bug Framing)**
Banned. This is when a writer takes something the reader sees as a negative (people scrolling, low hook rate, fewer viewers, lost prospects) and reframes it as the intended mechanism. It's a copywriter device. Real people don't talk like this.

Other names for the same pattern: reframe, objection reversal, feature-not-a-bug framing, problem-to-purpose reversal. Recognize all variants and kill them.

❌ "That's the filter doing its job."
❌ "That's not a problem. That's exactly how it's supposed to work."
❌ "That's a feature, not a bug."
❌ "That's the math working in your favor."
❌ "The auction picked."
❌ "The unqualified buyer scrolls. That's the filter doing its job."
❌ Any sentence that takes an apparent downside and recasts it as the intended outcome.
✅ State the observation directly without the reframe. "The unqualified buyer scrolls. That's expected — we built the hook to filter for the right audience." But ideally just stop the sentence after the observation. The reader can connect the dots.
✅ "Some prospects walked away after the audit. We were filtering them out anyway." Direct, no "that's the system working" closer.

The instinct to add the reframe usually means the writer wants to sound clever. Cut the clever line.

## The 60-Second Pre-Publish Checklist

Run every piece of AI-assisted content through this before publishing:

- [ ] Ctrl+F for colons. More than 2-3? Start deleting.
- [ ] Any "No X. No Y. No Z." patterns? Rewrite with commas.
- [ ] Does it start with "Most [people/founders]"? Delete it.
- [ ] Count "It's not X, it's Y" structures. More than one? Cut them.
- [ ] Any suspicious numbers (47, 73%, 37)? Replace with real data.
- [ ] Kill "powerful," "game-changing," "transformational."
- [ ] Read it out loud. Would you say this at a coffee shop?
- [ ] Is there anything that would make a commenter disagree?
- [ ] Any "That's the [reframe] doing its job" / "That's not a problem, that's the system working" lines? Cut them. State the observation, don't reframe it.

The fastest way to de-AI your writing is to add something that creates friction. AI avoids disagreement, while humans don't.
