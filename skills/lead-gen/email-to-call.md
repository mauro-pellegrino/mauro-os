## The Philosophy

Emails do not chase prospects. They show solutions and let the right people self-select.

The single most important rule in this system: every email gives insight, never value. Insight shows what is true and changes how the reader sees the problem. Value walks them through how to solve it. Giving value away for free means they solve it themselves and do not need to book a call. Giving insight means they know what the problem is but not how to execute the fix — that is what the call is for.

The recipe analogy: if you give someone the recipe, they cook it themselves. If you show them the finished dish, they want to buy it. Emails show the dish.

Educational content builds audiences. Insight-based content builds pipelines. This skill is for pipelines.

The Gold persona and the Big Brand persona will read the same email and see different things in it. That is the point. You are not writing to one person. You are showing one of Growthub's solutions clearly enough that whoever needs it recognises themselves.

The content is the sawdust, not the table. The table is results for clients. The sawdust is everything created along the way: the observation from an account review, the format that surprised everyone, the client question that revealed something worth sharing, the thing that worked last month and why.

Do not write about what Growthub does in the abstract. Write about what happened, what was noticed, what was learned. The insight comes from the story. The story comes from the work.

## The 5 Email Types

90% of emails fall into the first four. Sales emails are 10%.

**Problem** — Intercept a conversation the reader is already having in their head. Do not describe the problem clinically. Find the specific thought they are trying to put away and bring it to the surface. No solution. Make them feel seen.

**Outcome** — Show a result from client work. Specific numbers, specific context. Show, don't explain.

**Idea** — Share an observation, a reframe, a contrarian take from inside the work. One idea, fully explored.

**Leadership** — A position, a philosophy, a strong opinion about how things should be done. These build authority over time.

**Sales** — Direct offer to book a call. Used sparingly. Always specific about what happens on the call.

## Sawdust Bank: Where Emails Come From

Before writing, use these prompts to extract raw material. Write answers in one sentence each. Fast and unpolished. This is the source.

**From client work:**
- What problem did a client come to us with this month?
- What happened in an account review that was surprising?
- What format or angle worked that we did not expect?
- What did a client say about the results that was memorable?
- What did we try that failed, and what did we learn?
- What is the most common question we get on calls right now?
- What do brands in our pipeline have in common?
- What does a brand with creative fatigue actually look like day to day?
- What is one number from a client account that tells a story?

**From the business:**
- What did we decide to stop doing and why?
- What format are we producing this week that we were not producing six months ago?
- What is the hardest part of our process and why does it produce better results?
- What would most agencies do in this situation and what do we do instead?
- What does a winning ad look like vs what the client thought it would look like?

**From observations:**
- What is a brand doing wrong that we see constantly?
- What is the thing brands obsess over that does not actually move the needle?
- What do the brands that scale past $1M in monthly revenue have in common creatively?
- What do brands that plateau have in common?

Pick the idea that feels easiest to write about today. That is the right one.

## Insight vs Value — The Test

**Value** = walking the reader through how to do something. Step by step. Actionable. Teachable. This is what courses and guides do. It builds audiences. It does not build pipelines.

**Insight** = showing the reader something true that changes how they see the problem. You reveal what is happening and why it matters. You do not walk them through fixing it.

Example of value: "To fix your hook rate, start with the problem your customer is already thinking about, then introduce the product as the solution."

Example of insight: "The ads that tanked had great visuals. The ones that worked had the same visuals. The only thing that changed was the first sentence."

The reader gets the insight. They know something now they did not know before. But they do not know how to execute it. That is what the call is for.

The test: read the email back. Could someone copy-paste a method from it and go do it themselves? If yes, you have given value. Delete the how-to. Keep the what and the why.

## Email Structure

**Subject line**
Short, specific, no clickbait. Number or result if relevant. Reads like something a person would actually send.

Good: "The ad that took 20% of total spend"
Good: "What we noticed in every stalled account"
Good: "A question we keep getting"
Bad: "How to scale your brand with better creatives"
Bad: "The secret to winning ads on Meta"

**Body**
One thing. Remove everything that does not serve the one thing.

Open with the story or the observation. Not with context-setting, not with "I wanted to share." Get into it immediately.

Tell what happened honestly. Use numbers where most people would use adjectives. "One static took 20% of their total ad spend" not "one of the ads performed really well."

Share the insight. What does this story reveal? What would the reader do differently if they understood this? Give the insight. Do not walk them through every step of arriving at it.

Offer service. Never push to a purchase or a decision. Frame the call as the natural next step for whoever this email is relevant to. If the transition feels forced, put it in the P.S.

**P.S.**
Use when the CTA does not connect naturally to the body. "P.S. If your creative pipeline looks anything like what I described above, a pre-audit call is a good starting point. Link in bio / [link]."

## CTA Options (pick one, always at the end)

- "If this sounds like your account right now, book a free pre-audit call here: [link]"
- "We do a free pre-audit before anything else. It takes 20 minutes and you will leave knowing exactly what is holding your creative back. [link]"
- "Reply to this email if you want to talk through what this looks like for your brand."
- P.S. version when no natural transition exists

Never pitch the retainer in the email. The call is the offer.

## The Email Checklist

Before sending, run through this:

- [ ] Is it about one thing only? If not, cut everything that does not serve the one thing.
- [ ] Have I removed information that does not add to the one thing?
- [ ] Have I given insight, not value? The reader should know something new but not know how to execute it. If they can copy-paste a method from this email, delete the how-to. Educational content builds audiences. This email is for pipelines.
- [ ] Am I being honest? Go through every sentence and delete absolutes ("you must", "the best", "always", "never"). Replace vague statements with real numbers and specific outcomes. Honest marketing lowers defences.
- [ ] What is the natural next step? If there is a natural transition to the CTA, use it. If there is no natural transition, put it in the P.S. and do not force it.
- [ ] Is the CTA framed as a service, not a sale?
- [ ] Is there anything in here that could only come from Growthub's real experience?
- [ ] Run the voice.md pre-publish checklist

## Frequency

Daily is the goal. Weekly is the minimum. Consistency over volume.

The Gold persona will not read every email. They will read 3 of 7. That is still more exposure than a monthly newsletter.

Unsubscribes happen. The people who unsubscribe were not going to become clients.

---

## Red Velvet: How to Vary the CTA Without Writing New Offers

The pre-audit call is the same offer. Vary how it is framed:

- Limited spots this month
- Closing intake at end of quarter
- Reply-based (DM or email, no link)
- "We have one spot opening up next week"
- "We are doing free account reviews for the next 5 brands who respond"

Rotate these. The offer does not change. The framing does. Familiarity kills urgency.

## Example Email Structures

**Outcome email:**
```
Subject: [Specific result in plain language]

[1-2 sentences: what the situation was]
[2-3 sentences: what we did / what happened]
[1 sentence: the number that matters]
[2-3 sentences: what this reveals — the insight]
[CTA or P.S.]
```

**Problem email:**
```
Subject: [The problem, named directly]

[Open with the problem as it actually feels — not described clinically]
[2-3 sentences: why this is more common than people admit]
[1-2 sentences: what usually makes it worse]
[Insight: the thing most brands miss about this problem]
[CTA or P.S.]
```

**Idea email:**
```
Subject: [The reframe or contrarian take]

[Open with the observation — one thing noticed in the work]
[Expand: why this matters, what it changes]
[Concrete example or number]
[Closing line: what this means for the reader]
[CTA or P.S.]
```

## How to Run This Skill

**This skill requires input before writing anything. Do not invent stories, results, or observations. Every email must be grounded in something real that was provided.**

If no raw material has been provided, ask:

> "Give me something from the work this week. A client result, something that surprised you in an account, a question you keep getting on calls, something that worked or didn't. One sentence is enough to start."

Do not proceed until you have real input from the user.

---

**Step 1** — Receive the raw material from the user. This might be a client result, a number, an observation, a conversation, a thing that happened. Take it as given. Do not pad it out or invent context around it.

**Step 2** — Ask one follow-up question if something critical is missing (a number, the outcome, the context). Do not ask multiple questions.

**Step 3** — Identify the email type that fits the material (Problem, Outcome, Idea, Leadership, Sales).

**Step 4** — Write the subject line. Make it specific to the material provided.

**Step 5** — Write the body. Open with the story from the material. Share the insight it reveals. Offer the call.

**Step 6** — Run the checklist. Fix anything that fails.

**Step 7** — Check against voice.md anti-patterns. Kill em dashes, colon abuse, X/Y contrasts.
