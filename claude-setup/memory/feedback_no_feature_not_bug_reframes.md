Never write sentences that take something the reader sees as a downside (people scrolling, low hook rate, fewer viewers, lost prospects, an outcome that looks like a loss) and reframe it as the intended mechanism. This is a copywriter tic. Real people don't talk like this.

Pattern variants to recognize and kill:
- "That's the filter doing its job."
- "That's not a problem. That's exactly how it's supposed to work."
- "That's a feature, not a bug."
- "That's the math working in your favor."
- "The auction picked."
- "[Negative observation]. That's the system working."

Names for the pattern: reframe, objection reversal, feature-not-a-bug framing, problem-to-purpose reversal. All banned.

**Why:** Mauro caught this in two articles in one session ("That's the filter doing its job" appeared in the Hook System article and again in the 5-Formats article). He pointed out that this device adds nothing real — it's the writer wanting to sound clever. Voice.md now bans it as Pattern #9.

**How to apply:**
- State the observation directly. Stop the sentence after the observation. The reader connects the dots.
- If the reframe instinct shows up, cut the line entirely. The paragraph almost always reads better without it.
- Applies to all content: articles, autodm copy, case studies, lead magnets, posts.
- Project-level enforcement also lives in `growthub-os/brands/growthub/voice.md` Pattern #9 + the 60-second pre-publish checklist.
