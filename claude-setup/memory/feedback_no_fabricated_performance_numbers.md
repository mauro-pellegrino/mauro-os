Never invent specific performance numbers (hook rates, CPA drops, ROAS lifts, conversion rate lifts, AOV/LTV deltas, win rates) to make copy or lead magnets sound more compelling. Use only numbers that exist in:
- The source transcript or video
- Mauro's wins-log
- The X / LinkedIn / Calendly analytics files he provides
- A direct quote from a verified case

For any performance specific I can't ground in those sources, use a bracket placeholder like `[VERIFY]` or `[Add from wins-log]` so Mauro can fill in the real number before publishing.

**Why:** Mauro caught this three times in one session (skincare doc invented Cure pricing, app case study invented 30-50% CPA drop, Pixar autodm + doc invented "highest hook rates this year"). The qualified ICP will sniff fabricated numbers on a sales call and the trust ends. Voice.md Pattern #5 explicitly bans suspiciously specific numbers.

**How to apply:**
- Before writing any sentence with a percentage, dollar figure, or specific metric, ask: "Is this number in the transcript, wins-log, or data files I've been given?"
- If no, leave a bracket placeholder. Don't fill it in with a plausible-sounding extrapolation.
- This applies to lead magnets, case studies, autodm copy, articles, posts, and any other public-facing output.
- Acceptable to use directionally — "doubled," "more than tripled," "above the category average" — IF the directional claim is grounded in transcript or data. Specific percentages are not acceptable unless verified.
- Internal team summaries / weekly recaps can use my best-effort estimates with the estimate flagged as such.
